Narrative Probe tracks how news sources relate to consensus reality — the version of events most of the panel agrees on. It measures which outlets break stories early that later become the consensus view, which generate claims that never stick, and whose coverage most closely aligns with the mainstream account.

The system never says a source is "right" or "wrong." It shows you the data across six independent metrics. You decide which sources to trust.

Each dot is one source.
Y axis (Validation Rate): Of the claims a source originates first, what fraction were later corroborated by other independent sources? Higher = other outlets independently confirmed their scoops.
X axis (Origination Rate): How many unique claims does this source publish that no other outlet is simultaneously reporting? Higher = more original reporting. Click any dot to view that source

Coverage Analysis: Nakatomi Space Treaty

Report v2 — corpus update

Jun 1 – Sep 1, 2026 · 12 sources · 47 articles

Zone 1What the consensus says

Based on what the majority of the panel described, the Nakatomi Space Treaty negotiations opened on June 3 with competing frameworks for orbital resource rights. The Nakatomi delegation proposed a "first-use" allocation model granting priority claims to nations with existing orbital infrastructure. A coalition of 14 signatories countered with an "equitable access" framework requiring phased redistribution of orbital slots. By late July, a compromise emerged: a 5-year grandfather period followed by progressive reallocation. The treaty was provisionally adopted on August 28, pending ratification by the Nakatomi Federal Council.

A minority of sources (3 of 12) described the negotiations as collapsing on July 12, citing a diplomatic walkout by the Nakatomi delegation. This account was not reflected in the majority narrative after July 15.

Narrative Probe tracks consensus reality, not truth. This summary reflects the version of events described by the majority of the panel (≥9 of 12 sources).
