# How climate change is influencing Europe's record-breaking heat wave

**Source:** NPR, *Morning Edition*, June 24, 2026  
**Interview:** Michel Martin with Jennifer Francis, senior scientist at the Woodwell Climate Research Center (Massachusetts).  
**Audio length:** 5:02  

---

## Key Takeaways

- **Europe is warming faster than any other continent**, driven by specific ocean‑temperature patterns and jet‑stream behaviour.
- **Record‑breaking heat** has killed dozens; peak heat expected around June 24–25, 2026.
- **Ocean heat** is the immediate trigger: very warm waters around Western Europe (Mediterranean, eastern Atlantic off Spain/France, Arctic near Svalbard/Scandinavia) "prime the system" for summer heat waves.
- **Jet‑stream bulging** (a persistent pattern of cold water south of Greenland) forces warm tropical air north over Europe, creating a "heat dome". This pattern is repeating more frequently.
- **Adaptation is urgent** — many Europeans lack air conditioning; homes need better insulation, energy‑efficient cooling, and shifts in outdoor work schedules.
- **Americans face the same trajectory** — heat waves will become more extreme, longer, and more frequent everywhere.
- **The core remedy**: drastically reduce greenhouse‑gas emissions (burning fossil fuels, deforestation).

---

## Interview Summary

### 1. Causes of Europe's frequent heat emergencies

**Jennifer Francis** points to the oceans first:

> *"You really have to look at the oceans to understand why it's warming so fast in Europe. If you look at the water surrounding Western Europe right now … ocean temperatures are way above normal. … That very warm water there is sort of priming the system for having heat waves during the summertime."*

Additional amplifying factor: overall global warming shifts the baseline temperature range upward, increasing the odds of extreme heat anywhere.

### 2. Why Europe is warming faster than other continents

A persistent **ocean‑temperature contrast** drives a wavy jet stream:

> *"We're … seeing this area of colder ocean temperatures just south of Greenland. … When we have this cold water right up against the warm water, it tends to make the jet stream bulge … southward near Greenland and then up northward, up over Europe. … It allows the warm tropical air from the south to penetrate much farther north … This type of bulging heat dome … [is] happening more and more frequently."*

Thus, a stationary "heat dome" parked over Europe delivers prolonged extreme temperatures.

### 3. Adaptation strategies for Europe

- **Cooling infrastructure**: Europe is not accustomed to such heat; most homes/buildings lack air conditioning.
- **Behavioural changes**: shift outdoor work away from peak heat hours.
- **Building upgrades**: better insulation, adoption of energy‑efficient cooling (for those who can afford it).

### 4. Lessons for Americans

Michel Martin notes that American tourists already experience Europe's heat firsthand and return with stories.

Francis stresses that the underlying message is universal:

> *"The Earth is warming. We're going to see more and more of these record‑breaking heat waves … More frequently, the heat waves are going to be more extreme. They're going to last longer. So we need to adapt. We need to insulate our homes better. If we can afford air conditioning, put that in, and look for more energy-efficient ways to do it as well."*

The same dynamics are already fuelling wildfires in the U.S. West (mentioned as "previous segment").

### 5. The single most important action right now

> *"Right now, we have to focus with a laser on reducing the amount of emissions of carbon gases that we're putting in the atmosphere. This is treating the disease, the underlying disease. And of course, those emissions are mainly from burning fossil fuels, but it's also from cutting down forests."*
